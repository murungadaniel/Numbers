    Subsection 1.4.2 Total value of digits in decimals

    Activity 1.4.2.

    1. Working in groups, think of a number that has \(5\) decimal points i.e \(18.52847,7.24385\) and in turns write it down.
    2. Discuss the chosen decimal numbers with fellow group members.
    3. Draw a place value chart and write the decimal numbers on the chart.
    4. Identify the place value of each digit on the chart then identify the total value of each digits.
    5. With your fellow learners, discuss how to identify the total value of digits in a decimal number.

    Subsection 1.4.3 Multiplying decimals by a whole number

    Activity 1.4.3.

    1. Measure the length and width of your classroom in cm using a meter ruler and write it down.
    2. Discuss the measurement of one side of your classroom and convert the measurement to \(m\) i.e \(1 m = 100 cm \text{.}\)
    3. Multiply the length and width in \(m\) by the number of sides in your classroom. What do you get?
    4. How many classrooms do you have in your school?
    5. Suppose all the classrooms has same lengths and widths, determine the total measurement of both length and width of classrooms in your school.
    6. How did you determine the total measurement of length and width of classrooms in the school?

    Example 1.4.5.

    Work out:
    1. \(\displaystyle 7.503 \times 10\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 7.503 \times 100\)
    Solution.
    1. \(\displaystyle 7.503 \times 10\)
      \begin{align*} 7.503 \times 10 \amp = \frac{7503}{1000} \times 10 \\ \\ \amp = \frac{75030}{1000} = 75.03 \end{align*}
      When you multiply a decimal by 10, the decimal point moves \(1\) place to the right.
    2. \(\displaystyle 7.503 \times 100\)
      \begin{align*} 7.503 \times 100 \amp = \frac{7503}{1000} \times 100\\ \\ \amp = \frac{750300}{1000} = 750.3 \end{align*}
      When you multiply a decimal by 100, the decimal point moves \(2\) places to the right.

    Example 1.4.6.

    The mass of a piece of wood is \(12.463\) kg. What is the mass of \(12\) such pieces of wood?
    Solution.
    \(12463\)
    \(\times \, 12\)
    \(24926\) \(\to\) \(12463 \times 2\)
    \(+\,124630\) \(\to\) \(12463 \times 10\)
    \(149556\)
    1. Identify the number of decimal places.
      \(12.463\) has \(3\) decimal places.
    2. Multiply \(12463\) by \(12.\)
    3. In the product, count \(3\) digits from the right towards the left and insert the decimal point.
    \(\qquad \qquad \quad = 149.556\)
    \(12.463 \times 12 = 149.556\)

    Example 1.4.11.

    Work out: \(4.75 \div 5\)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} 4.75 \div 5 \amp = \frac{475}{100} \div 5\\ \\ \amp = \frac{475}{100} \times \frac{1}{5}\\ \amp \\ \amp \qquad 95\\ \amp = \frac{\cancel{475}}{\cancel{500}}\\ \amp \qquad 100\\ \amp \\ \amp = 0.95 \end{align*}
    1. Make the dividend to a fraction.
    2. Multiply the dividend by the reciprocal of the divisor.

    Example 1.4.13.

    Erick had a sugarcane whose length was \(6.24\) m. He cut the sugarcane into equal pieces of \(1.04\) m. How many pieces did he get?
    Solution.
    \(6.24 \text{ m } \div 1.04 \text{ m } = \frac{6.24}{1.04}\)
    Multiply by \(100\) to make the divisor a whole number.
    \begin{align*} \frac{6.24 \times 100}{1.04 \times 100} \amp = \frac{624}{104}\\ \amp \\ \amp = 6 \end{align*}
    Erick got \(6\) pieces.
