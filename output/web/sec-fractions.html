    Section 1.3 Fractions

    Fractions play a key role in our daily lives. For example, Onyimbo, a father of four, bought an orange for Ksh 50 and wanted to share it equally among his children. To ensure fairness, he first cut the orange into two equal halves. Then, he divided each half into two more equal parts, resulting in four equal pieces. Each child received one piece, showing how fractions help in dividing things equally.

    Subsection 1.3.1 Arranging fractions in ascending order

    Activity 1.3.1.

    Work in groups
    1. Make circular paper cutouts as the ones shown below.
    2. Arrange the circles paper in order, starting with the one that has the smallest colored part and ending with the one that has the largest colored part.
    3. Identify the fractions that correspond to the shaded parts in the ordered sequence of circles.
    Share your work with learners in the class.
    Ascending order means from the smallest to the largest.
    To arrange fractions in ascending order, first convert them into their equivalent fractions using the same denominator.

    Example 1.3.1.

    1. Arrange the following fractions in ascending order.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3},\frac{2}{5},\frac{3}{4},\frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    1. Find the LCM of \(3,5,4 \text{ and } 2.\)
      2 3 5 4 2
      2 3 5 2 1
      3 3 5 1 1
      5 1 5 1 1
      1 1 1 1
      \(2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 60\)
      The LCM of \(3,5,4 \text{ and } 2 \text{ is } 60.\)
    2. Rewrite the fractions to have a common denominator.
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{3} \amp = \frac{1 \times 20}{3 \times 20} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{20}{60} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} \frac{2}{5} \amp = \frac{2 \times 12}{5 \times 12} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{24}{60} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} \frac{3}{4} \amp = \frac{3 \times 15}{ 4 \times 15} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{45}{60} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} \amp = \frac{1 \times 30}{2 \times 30} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{30}{60} \end{align*}
    3. Arrange the fractions in ascending order.
      \(\frac{20}{60},\frac{24}{60},\frac{30}{60},\frac{45}{60}\)
      \(\frac{1}{3},\frac{2}{5},\frac{1}{2},\frac{3}{4}\)

    Example 1.3.2.

    Arrange the following fractions in ascending order.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{5}, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{7}{10} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    1. Convert each fraction into a percentage.
      \(\frac{1}{4} \times 100 = 25\%\)
      \(\frac{3}{5} \times 100 = 60\%\)
      \(\frac{1}{2} \times 100 = 50\%\)
      \(\frac{7}{10} \times 100 = 70\%\)
    2. Arrange the percentages in ascending order.
      \(25\%, 50\%, 60\%, 70\%\)
      The correct order is \(\frac{1}{4},\frac{1}{2},\frac{3}{5},\frac{7}{10}.\)

    Subsection 1.3.2 Arranging fractions in descending order

    Activity 1.3.2.

    Work in groups
    1. Make circular paper cutouts as the ones shown below.
    2. Arrange the circles paper in order, starting with the one that has the largest colored part and ending with the one that has the smallest colored part.
    3. Identify the fractions representing the shaded parts in the way you have arranged the colored part from the largest to the smallest colored part.
    Descending order means from the largest to the smallest.
    To arrange fractions in descending order, rewrite the fractions to have the same denominator.

    Example 1.3.4.

    Arrange the following fractions in descending order.
    \(\frac{1}{12},\frac{2}{3},\frac{5}{6},\frac{9}{24}\)
    Solution.
    1. Find the LCM of \(3,6,12 \text{ and } 24.\)
      2 3 6 12 24
      2 3 2 6 12
      2 3 1 3 6
      3 3 1 3 3
      1 1 1 1
      \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 3 = 24\)
      The LCM of \(3,6,12 \text{ and } 24 \text{ is } 24.\)
    2. Rewrite the fractions to have a common denominator.
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{12} \amp = \frac{1 \times 2}{12 \times 2} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{2}{24} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} \frac{5}{6} \amp = \frac{5 \times 4}{6 \times 4} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{20}{24} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} \amp = \frac{2 \times 8}{ 3 \times 8} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{16}{24} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} \frac{9}{24} \amp = \frac{9 \times 1}{24 \times 1} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{9}{24} \end{align*}
    3. Arrange the fractions in descending order.
      \(\frac{20}{24},\frac{16}{24},\frac{9}{24},\frac{2}{24}\)
      \(\frac{5}{6},\frac{2}{3},\frac{9}{24},\frac{1}{12}\)

    Example 1.3.5.

    Arrange the following fractions in descending order.
    \(\frac{2}{5},\frac{9}{10},\frac{3}{4},\frac{7}{20}\)
    Solution.
    1. Convert each fraction into a percentage.
      \(\frac{2}{5} \times 100 = 40\%\)
      \(\frac{9}{10} \times 100 = 90\%\)
      \(\frac{3}{4} \times 100 = 75\%\)
      \(\frac{7}{20} \times 100 = 35\%\)
    2. Arrange the percentages in descending order.
      \(90\%, 75\%, 40\%, 35\%\)
      The correct order is \(\frac{9}{10},\frac{3}{4},\frac{2}{5},\frac{7}{20}.\)

    Subsection 1.3.3 Adding proper fractions

    Activity 1.3.3.

    Work in groups
    1. Make a circular paper cut-out and divide it into 8 equal parts as shown below and shaded two parts as the one colored below.
    2. What fraction of the whole circle do the colored parts a and b represent?
    3. Combine together the two colored parts. Which fraction of the whole circle do the combined colored parts represent?

    Example 1.3.7.

    Add: \(\frac{3}{8} + \frac{5}{9}\)
    Solution.
    1. Find the LCM of \(8 \text{ and }9.\)
      2 8 9
      2 4 9
      2 2 9
      3 1 9
      3 1 3
      1 1
      \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 = 72\)
      The LCM of \(8 \text{ and } 9 \text{ is } 72.\)
    2. Rewrite the fractions to have a common denominator.
      \begin{align*} \frac{3}{8} \amp = \frac{3 \times 9}{8 \times 9} \\ \amp = \frac{27}{72} \\ \frac{5}{9} \amp = \frac{5 \times 8}{9 \times 8} \\ \amp = \frac{40}{72} \end{align*}
    3. Add the fractions.
      \begin{align*} \frac{27}{72} + \frac{40}{72} = \frac{67}{72} \amp \\ \text{ Therefore }\\ \frac{3}{8} + \frac{5}{9} \amp = \frac{67}{72} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.3.4 Adding mixed fractions

    Activity 1.3.4.

    Work in groups
    Juma’s family was expecting visitors that evening. In preparation, Juma’s mother bought \(1 \frac{1}{2}\) kg of meat. Later, his father bought an additional \(2 \frac{1}{4}\) kg of meat to ensure there was enough for everyone. How much meat did they have altogether?
    Share your work with other learners in the class.

    Example 1.3.9.

    Work out: \(7 \frac{1}{4} + 5 \frac{1}{3}\)
    Solution.
    1. Convert the mixed fractions into improper fractions.
      \begin{align*} 7 \frac{1}{4} \amp = \frac{4 \times 7 + 1}{4} = \frac{29}{4} \\ \\ 5 \frac{1}{3} \amp = \frac{3 \times 5 +1}{3} = \frac{16}{3} \end{align*}
    2. Add the improper fractions using LCM.
      \begin{align*} \frac{29}{4} + \frac{16}{3} = \frac{(3 \times 29)+(4 \times 16)}{12} \amp = \frac{87+64}{12} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{151}{12} = 12\frac{7}{12} \\ \\ \text{Therefore } 7 \frac{1}{4} + 5 \frac{1}{3} \amp = 12 \frac{7}{12} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.3.5 Subtracting proper fractions

    Example 1.3.11.

    Work out: \(\frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{5}\)
    Solution.
    1. Find the LCM of the denominators.
      LCM of the denominators is \(15.\)
    2. Rewrite the fractions to have a common denominator.
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{3} \amp = \frac{1 \times 5}{3 \times 5} \\ \amp = \frac{5}{15} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{5} \amp = \frac{1 \times 3}{5 \times 3} \\ \amp = \frac{3}{15} \end{align*}
    3. Subtract.
      \begin{equation*} \frac{5}{15} - \frac{3}{15} = \frac{2}{15} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{5} = \frac{2}{15} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.3.6 Subtracting mixed fractions

    Example 1.3.13.

    Work out: \(23 \frac{1}{2} - 12 \frac{1}{4} = \)
    Solution.
    1. Convert the fractions into improper fractions.
      \begin{align*} 23 \frac{1}{2} \amp = \frac{2 \times 23 + 1}{2} \\ \amp = \frac{47}{2} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} 12 \frac{1}{4} \amp = \frac{4 \times 12 + 1}{4} \\ \amp = \frac{49}{4} \end{align*}
    2. Subtract the improper fractions using LCM.
      \begin{align*} \frac{47}{2} - \frac{49}{4} \amp = \frac{94-49}{4} \\ \amp = \frac{45}{4} = 11 \frac{1}{4} \end{align*}

    Activity 1.3.5.

Each cake, according to the recipe, requires a \(\frac{1}{2}\) kg of sugar. Otuoma wonders the total amount of sugar he will need in total?.     Example 1.3.15.

    Work out: \(\frac{8}{9} \times 5 \)
    Solution.
    1. Multipy the numerator by \(5.\)
      \begin{align*} \frac{8}{9} \times 5 \amp = \frac{8 \times 5}{9}\\ \\ \amp = \frac{40}{9} \end{align*}
    2. Convert the improper fraction into a mixed fraction.
      \begin{align*} \frac{40}{9} \amp = 4 \frac{4}{9} \\ \\ \frac{8}{9} \times 5 \amp = 4\frac{4}{9} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.3.8 Multiplying a fraction by a fraction

    Example 1.3.17.

    Work out: \(\frac{3}{5} \times \frac{7}{9}\)
    Solution.
    Multiply the numerators and the denominators separately.
    \begin{align*} \frac{3}{5} \times \frac{7}{9} \amp = \frac{3 \times 7}{5 \times 9}\\ \amp = \frac{21}{45} \end{align*}
    simplify the answer
    \begin{align*} \frac{21}{45} \amp = \frac{21 \div 3}{45 \div 3} \\ \amp = \frac{7}{15} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.3.9 Multiplying a fraction by a mixed number

    Example 1.3.19.

    Work out: \(\frac{2}{3} \times 2 \frac{1}{7} \)
    Solution.
    1. Convert \(2 \frac{1}{7}\) to improper fraction.
      \begin{align*} 2 \frac{1}{7} \amp = \frac{7 \times 2 +1}{7} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{15}{7} \end{align*}
    2. Multiply the fractions.
      \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{15}{7} \amp = \frac{2 \times 15}{3 \times 7}\\ \\ \amp = \frac{30}{21} \end{align*}
    3. Simplify
      \begin{align*} \frac{30 \div 3}{21 \div 3} \amp = \frac{10}{7}\\ \\ \frac{10}{7} \amp = 1 \frac{3}{7} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.3.10 Reciprocal of fractions

    1. To find the reciprocal of a fraction, swap the numerator and the denominator.
    2. The product of a fraction and its reciprocal is \(1.\)

    Example 1.3.21.

    What is the reciprocal of \(\frac{11}{23}\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    To get the reciprocal of \(\frac{11}{23}\text{,}\) we swap the numerator and the denominator.
    \(\frac{11}{23} \text{ to get } \frac{23}{11}\)
    The the reciprocal of \(\frac{11}{23} \text{ is } \frac{23}{11}.\)

    Example 1.3.22.

    What is the reciprocal of \(6 \frac{4}{5}\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    1. Convert \(6 \frac{4}{5}\) into an improper fraction.
      \(6 \frac{4}{5} = \frac{(5 \times 6)+4}{3 \times 7} = \frac{34}{5}\)
    2. Swap the numerator and the denominator.
      \(\frac{34}{5} \text{ to get } \frac{5}{34}\)
      The reciprocal of \(6 \frac{4}{5} \text{ is } \frac{5}{34}.\)

    Subsection 1.3.11 Division of a fraction by a whole number

    When dividing a fraction by a whole number, multiply the fraction by the reciprocal of the whole.

    Example 1.3.24.

    Work out: \(\frac{3}{20} \div 6 \)
    Solution.
    1. Find the reciprocal of the divisor.
      The reciprocal of \(6\) is \(\frac{1}{6}.\)
    2. Multiply.
      \begin{align*} \frac{3}{20} \times \frac{1}{6} \amp = \frac{3 \times 1}{20 \times 6}\\ \\ \amp = \frac{3}{120} \\ \\ \frac{3}{120} \amp = \frac{3 \div 3}{120 \div 3} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{1}{40} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.3.14 Division of a fraction by a whole number

    Activity 1.3.8.

    Example 1.3.30.

    Work out: \(20 \div \frac{1}{5} = \)
    Solution.
    1. Find the reciprocal of the divisor.
      The reciprocal of \(\frac{1}{5}\) is \(\frac{5}{1}.\)
    2. Multiply \(20\) by the reciprocal of \(\frac{1}{5}.\)
      \begin{align*} 20 \div \frac{1}{5} \amp = 20 \times \frac{5}{1} \\ \amp = 100 \\ 20 \frac{1}{5} \amp = 100 \end{align*}

    Evaluate: \(35 \div 3 \frac{1}{2} = \)
    Solution.
    1. Convert \(3 \frac{1}{2}\) into an improper fraction.
      \begin{align*} 3 \frac{1}{2} \amp = \frac{2 \times 3 + 1}{2} \\ \\ \amp = \frac{7}{2} \end{align*}
    2. Find the reciprocal of \(\frac{7}{2}.\)
      The reciprocal of \(\frac{7}{2}\) is \(\frac{2}{7}\)
    3. Multiply \(35\) by \(\frac{2}{7}.\)
      \begin{align*} 35 \times \frac{2}{7} \amp = \frac{70}{7}\\ \\ \amp = 10 \\ 35 \div 3 \frac{1}{2} \amp = 10 \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.3.15 Number sequence involving fractions

    Activity 1.3.9.

    Work in groups
    1. Observe the pattern in the following sequence of fractions: \(\frac{1}{4},\frac{1}{9},\frac{1}{16},\frac{1}{25}\)
    2. Identify the pattern in these fractions.
    3. Determine the next fraction in the sequence.
    4. Share your findings with your classmates.
    Hint.
    Identify the pattern formed by numerators and denominators.

    Example 1.3.33.

    Find the next fraction in the sequence.
    \(\frac{1}{4},\frac{1}{9},\frac{1}{16},\frac{1}{25},\frac{1}{36},\)
    Solution.
    1. The denominators are squares of numbers \(2,3,4,5 \text{ and } 6.\)
    2. The next denominator is the square of \(7.\)
      \(7 \times 7 = 49\)
    3. The next fraction in the sequence is \(\frac{1}{49}.\)
      The sequence is \(\frac{1}{4},\frac{1}{9},\frac{1}{16},\frac{1}{25},\frac{1}{36},\\frac{1}{49}.\)

    Example 1.3.34.

    What is the next fraction in the pattern ?
    Solution.
    1. Convert the mixed fractions into improper fractions.
      \(7 \frac{1}{2} = \frac{15}{2}, \,\, 3 \frac{3}{4} = \frac{15}{4}, \,\, 1\frac{7}{8} = \frac{15}{8}\)
    2. Rewrite the sequence.
      \(\frac{15}{2},\frac{15}{4},\frac{15}{8},\frac{15}{16}\)
      The denominator is the multiplied by two to get the next fraction.
      \(\frac{15}{16 \times 2} = \frac{15}{32}\)
      The next fraction is \(\frac{15}{32}.\)
      The sequence is \(7 \frac{1}{2},3\frac{3}{4},1\frac{7}{8},\frac{15}{16},\frac{15}{32}.\)

    Subsection 1.3.16 Creating number sequences involving fractions

    Example 1.3.36.

    Create a subtraction sequence of four fractions starting from \(2 \frac{1}{4}.\)
    Solution.
    Rule: Subtract the same fraction repeatedly to get the next number.
    \begin{align*} 2 \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4} \amp = 2 \\ \\ 2 - \frac{1}{4} \amp = 1 \frac{3}{4} \\ \\ 1 \frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{4} \amp = 1 \frac{2}{4} = 1 \frac{1}{2} \\ \end{align*}
    The sequence is \(2\frac{1}{4},2,1\frac{3}{4},1\frac{1}{2}.\)
