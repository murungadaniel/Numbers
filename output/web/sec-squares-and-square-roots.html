    Section 1.5 Squares and square roots

    Sheilah and Murunga
    Squares and square roots are fundamental concepts in mathematics, with many applications in our daily lives.
    Consider a surveyor tasked with determining the side length of a square garden with an area of \(400\) m\(^2\text{.}\) Since all sides of a square are equal, the surveyor needs to find a number that, when multiplied by itself, results in \(400\text{.}\) This is where the concept of square roots comes into play.
    To find the side length of the garden, the surveyor needs to determine the square root of \(400\text{.}\) In this case, the square root of \(400\) is \(20\) since \(20 \times 20 = 400\text{,}\) meaning each side of the garden is \(20\) m long.
    We can represent this mathematically as follows:
    • Area of a square = side length \(\times\) side length
    • \(a^2 = a \times a\) , where \(a\) represents the side length of the square.

    Subsection 1.5.1 Squares of whole numbers

    Activity 1.5.1.

    Work in groups
    1. Draw a grid of squares like the one shown below.
    2. Start by counting all the smallest squares \((1 \times 1)\) in the grid.
    3. Then, identify and count all the larger squares, such as \(2 \times 2, \,\,\) \(3 \times 3\text{,}\) and so on, until you reach the largest square that fits within the grid.
    4. Write down the number of squares for each size \((e.g., 1 \times 1, \,\, 2 \times 2, ...) \) in a table for better organization. Add them up to find the total number of squares in the grid.
    5. Discuss your findings with a in a small group. Compare how many squares each of you found and check for any missed squares.
    6. Think about the process you used to count the squares. Could there be a quicker way to calculate the total number of squares without counting each one individually?

    Example 1.5.3.

    The length of a square flowerbed is \(43\) m. What is the area of the flowerbed?
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} \text{Area } \amp = \text{ Length } \times \text{Length} \\ \amp = 43 \text{ m } \times 43 \text{ m } \\ \amp = 1\,849 \text{m}^2 \end{align*}
    \(43\)
    \(\times 43\)
    \(129\) \(43 \times 3\)
    \(+ 1720\) \(43 \times 40\)
    \(1849\)
    The area of the flowerbed is \(1\,849 \text{ m}^2.\)

    Subsection 1.5.2 Sqaures of fractions

    Activity 1.5.2.

    Activity for squares of fractions goes in here.

    Example 1.5.5.

    Work out the square of:
    1. \(\displaystyle \frac{3}{4}\)
    Solution.
    1. \(\left( \frac{3}{4} \right)^2\)
      Square the numerator and the denominator.
      \begin{align*} \left( \frac{3}{4} \right)^2 \amp = \frac{3 \times 3}{4 \times 4} \\ \amp = \frac{9}{16} \end{align*}

    Example 1.5.6.

    Work out the following:
    1. \(\displaystyle \left(1 \frac{1}{2}\right)^2\)
    Solution.
    \(\left(1 \frac{1}{2}\right)^2\)
    Convert the mixed fraction to an improper fraction.
    \begin{equation*} 1\frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{2} \end{equation*}
    Find the square of the improper fraction.
    \begin{align*} \left(1 \frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \amp = \frac{3 \times 3}{2 \times 2}\\ \amp = \frac{9}{4} \end{align*}
    Convert the improper fraction to a mixed fraction.
    \begin{align*} \frac{9}{4} \amp = 2 \frac{1}{4} \\ \amp = 2 \frac{1}{4} \end{align*}

    Example 1.5.10.

    Find the square root of \(324\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Express \(324\) as a product of its prime factors and pair up similar factors together.
    \begin{align*} 324 \amp = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3\\ \sqrt{324} \amp = \sqrt{2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3} \end{align*}
    select one factor from each pair and multiply the selected factors.
    \begin{align*} \amp = 2 \times 3 \times 3 \\ \amp = 18 \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.5.5 Square roots of fractions

    Activity 1.5.5.

    Activity for square roots of fractions goes here.
    To find the square root of a fraction, work out the square root of the numerator and square root of the denominator separately.

    Example 1.5.12.

    Find the square root of \(\frac{25}{81}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{\frac{25}{81}} \amp = \frac{\sqrt{25}}{\sqrt{81}} \\ \amp = \frac{\sqrt{5 \times 5}}{\sqrt{9 \times 9}} \\ \amp = \frac{5}{9} \end{align*}

    Example 1.5.13.

    Work out: \(\sqrt{11\frac{1}{9}}\)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} 11 \frac{1}{9} \amp = \frac{9 \times 11 + 1}{9} \\ \amp = \frac{100}{9} \\ \sqrt{11 \frac{1}{9}} \amp = \frac{\sqrt{100}}{\sqrt{9}} \\ \frac{\sqrt{10 \times 10}}{\sqrt{3 \times 3}} \amp = \frac{10}{3}\\ \amp = \frac{10}{3}\\ \amp = 3 \frac{1}{3} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.5.6 Square roots of decimals

    Activity 1.5.6.

    Activity for square roots of decimals

    Example 1.5.15.

    1. Work out: \(\sqrt{12.96}\)
    2. Work out: \(\sqrt{2.25}\)
    Solution.
    1. \(\sqrt{12.96}\)
      \begin{align*} \sqrt{12.96} \amp = \sqrt{\frac{1296}{100}} \\ \amp = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3}{2 \times 2 \times 5 \times 5}} \\ \amp = \frac{2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3}{2 \times 5} = \frac{36}{10} \\ \amp= 3.6 \end{align*}
    2. \(\sqrt{2.25}\)
      \begin{align*} \sqrt{2.25} \amp = \sqrt{\frac{225}{100}} \\ \amp = \sqrt{\frac{3 \times 3 \times 5 \times 5}{2 \times 2 \times 5 \times 5}} \\ \amp = \frac{5 \times 3}{2 \times 5} = \frac{15}{10} \\ \amp = 3.6 \end{align*}
